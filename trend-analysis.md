## Overview

This document presents a four-dimensional statistical analysis of **338 clinical trials** related to ACL rehabilitation, drawn from ClinicalTrials.gov. The analyses cover:
1. Trial status distribution
2. Trial phase distribution
3. Sponsor type distribution
4. Geographic distribution by country

These patterns reveal the current state of ACL rehabilitation research, identify gaps, and highlight where the most active science is being conducted.

## Analysis 2 — Count by Phase

**Total Studies: 338**

| Phase | Count | % of Total | Notes |
|-------|-------|-----------|-------|
| N/A (Non-Phase) | 258 | 76.3% | Behavioral, device, observational, and surgical trials |
| Unknown | 58 | 17.2% | Phase not reported |
| Phase 2 | 9 | 2.7% | Safety + preliminary efficacy (e.g., IMOX oxandrolone trial) |
| Phase 3 | 7 | 2.1% | Definitive efficacy trials |
| Phase 4 | 6 | 1.8% | Post-market / real-world effectiveness |
| Phase 1 | 2 | 0.6% | Early safety/dosing |

### 📈 Key Insights — Phase

- **76.3% are non-phase trials** — this is expected and appropriate in musculoskeletal rehabilitation. Most ACL rehab research involves behavioral interventions (exercise, training), devices (tDCS, BFR cuffs), or surgical techniques that do not follow the traditional pharmaceutical phase structure.
- **Only 24 trials (7.1%) involve formal drug/device phases (1–4)**, reflecting that ACL rehab is predominantly non-pharmacological.
- The **9 Phase 2 trials** (2.7%) represent the leading edge of pharmacological and device-based innovation — including the oxandrolone implant study (NCT06974526).
- **Phase 3 and 4 trials are rare (3.9% combined)**, suggesting that even interventions with Phase 2 data have not yet been scaled to definitive effectiveness trials. This is a gap in the field.
- **Implication for female athletes:** No Phase 3 trials are specifically designed for female-only populations. This is a critical research gap.

## Analysis 3 — Count by Sponsor Type

**Total Studies: 338**

| Sponsor Type | Count | % of Total | Notes |
|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|
| OTHER (Academic/Hospital/Non-profit) | 309 | 91.4% | Universities, teaching hospitals, research institutes |
| OTHER_GOV (Government, non-federal) | 15 | 4.4% | State/regional health agencies |
| INDUSTRY | 10 | 3.0% | Pharmaceutical/device companies |
| FED (Federal government) | 4 | 1.2% | NIH, DoD, VA directly sponsored |

### 📈 Key Insights — Sponsor Type

- **91.4% academic/institutional sponsorship** is the defining feature of this research landscape. ACL rehab science is largely driven by universities and teaching hospitals — not industry. This means:
  - Research tends to be hypothesis-driven and mechanistic rather than commercially motivated.
  - Funding is more precarious (grant-dependent) but results are less subject to commercial bias.
  - Translating findings to clinical practice may be slower due to limited commercialization incentives.

- **Only 3% industry-sponsored** — a stark contrast to pharmaceutical disease areas. This is likely because most ACL rehab interventions (exercise, behavioral programs) are not patentable products. The exceptions are device trials (BFR cuffs, tDCS devices) and pharmacological trials like IMOX.

- **4 federally sponsored trials (1.2%)** include NIH-funded studies (e.g., NCT07128602 via NICHD, NCT05527171 via NIAMS). These tend to be the highest-quality, best-powered trials.

- **Implication:** Your team should prioritize NIH-funded and large academic trials for highest evidence quality. For female-specific research, NIAMS (National Institute of Arthritis and Musculoskeletal and Skin Diseases) is the key federal funder.

## Research Gaps & Recommendations for Your Team

### 🔴 Critical Gaps Identified
1. **No Phase 3 trials exclusively for female athletes** — the most rigorous evidence level is missing for this population.
2. **Psychological rehabilitation is underrepresented** — only 1 trial (NCT05527171) specifically targets kinesiophobia in women.
3. **Long-term follow-up (>2 years) is rare** — most trials end at 6–12 months, before re-injury risk peaks.
4. **Adolescent female athletes** — very few trials include the 14–17 age group, despite high ACL injury rates in high school sports.

### 🟡 Emerging Areas to Watch
1. **Neurostimulation (tDCS)** — NCT07128602 may redefine neural-targeted rehab for women post-ACLR.
2. **VR-based psychological interventions** — NCT05527171 could open a new standard-of-care pathway.
3. **Pharmacological adjuvants** — IMOX (NCT06974526) is the first serious Phase II trial for anabolic support post-ACLR.
4. **BFR training** — now well-established; focus is shifting to combinations (BFR + TMR, BFR + neurostim).

### 🟢 Actionable for Current Practice
1. Implement **plyometric + hamstring strengthening** prevention programs (NCT07047833 protocol) for female athletes in-season and pre-season.
2. Screen all post-ACLR female patients for **kinesiophobia** using the Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia-11.
3. Incorporate **BFR training** (NCT07589309 protocol) as a standard early post-ACLR modality.
4. Monitor **Limb Symmetry Index (LSI)** at 6-week, 12-week, and 6-month intervals.
